# Section 6 — Cross-functional Coordination & CAPEX Governance

## 6.1 Multi-product CDMO: Maximum organizational complexity

A CDMO site like Lonza Visp simultaneously operates multiple production lines for several pharmaceutical clients. This multi-product/multi-client reality imposes cross-functional coordination of complexity far greater than a dedicated single-product site.

The equipment engineer operates at the intersection of four functions:

```
         Engineering
              |
Operations ——SME——  Quality
              |
           Validation
```

Each function has its own priorities, its own language, its own constraints. The ability to operate simultaneously in these four languages is the central organizational competency of the role.

## 6.2 Front-loading collaboration in CAPEX

The pharmaceutical project management literature converges on one principle: **the cost of correcting an error increases exponentially with project advancement**. An error identified in the design phase costs 1 — the same error identified in the OQ phase costs 100-1000.

Front-loading consists of investing heavily in multi-disciplinary reviews in early phases (URS, design review, FAT) to avoid late-stage failures. The equipment SME is the guardian of this discipline within their scope.

## 6.3 CAPEX governance tools

- **Design Review**: formal multi-disciplinary meeting at each milestone (concept, preliminary, final)
- **Action Item Tracking**: list of open points with owner and deadline
- **Change Management**: any deviation from approved design goes through a formal process
- **Lessons Learned**: systematic capitalization for subsequent projects

## 6.4 Shared KPIs as common language

Creating shared KPIs between Engineering, Quality and Operations reduces organizational friction:

| KPI | Engineering | Quality | Operations |
|-----|-------------|---------|------------|
| OEE | Equipment availability | Batch quality | Production performance |
| CAPA closure rate | Technical fixes | QMS compliance | Incident reduction |
| PM compliance | Plan completion rate | Deviation risk | Availability |
| Qualification on-time | Project delivery | Inspection readiness | Ramp-up planning |

# Section 7 — Safety, Health & Environment (SHE) in HPAPI/Biologics context

## 7.1 SHE as integrated dimension of the engineer role

In an HPAPI/biologics environment, safety is not an additional constraint — it's an intrinsic component of each engineering activity. The equipment engineer is a safety actor, not a simple observer.

## 7.2 Hierarchy of controls

```
1. Elimination              → Substitute HPAPI if possible
2. Substitution             → Change the process
3. Engineering controls     → Containment, isolation, ventilation ← SME domain
4. Administrative controls  → Procedures, training, permits
5. PPE                      → Last line of defense
```

The equipment engineer operates primarily at level 3 — engineering controls are their direct domain.

## 7.3 Risk assessment in HPAPI maintenance

Before any maintenance intervention in HPAPI zones, a formal risk assessment is required:

- Identification of substances present (and their banding)
- Assessment of potential exposure (duration, route)
- Definition of required PPE
- Preliminary decontamination procedures
- Emergency plan (spill, accidental exposure)

## 7.4 Environment and sustainability

Modern pharmaceutical sites integrate environmental objectives into their operations. For the equipment engineer:
- Energy optimization of utilities (HVAC, hot water, steam)
- HPAPI effluent management (treatment before discharge)
- Maintenance waste reduction (spare parts optimization, reconditioning)
- Environmental reporting (energy consumption, waste)
