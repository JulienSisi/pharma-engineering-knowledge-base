# Section 4 — Interdepartmental coordination: Utilities, Process & Calibration

## 4.1 Coordination as a core competency

In a pharmaceutical GMP environment, maintenance is never an isolated activity. It operates within a network of functional dependencies involving at least three operational departments:

- **Utilities**: production and distribution of process fluids (compressed air, purified water, steam, HVAC)
- **Process**: API production, with its batch scheduling constraints
- **Calibration**: maintenance of metrology and measurement instruments in compliance

Coordination between these three functions — often with partially divergent objectives — is a critical organizational competency that maintenance performance models recognize as determinant for overall site efficiency.

## 4.2 TPM model and transverse involvement

Total Productive Maintenance (TPM) — developed by Nakajima (1988) and formalized by the Japan Institute of Plant Maintenance — is the reference framework for maintenance integrated into the production organization. Its eight pillars explicitly include **autonomous maintenance** (operator involvement) and **planned maintenance** (coordination between maintenance and production).

```
The 8 TPM pillars:
1. Autonomous maintenance (operators)
2. Planned maintenance (maintenance)
3. Focused improvement (Kaizen)
4. Education & skills development
5. Early management (new equipment/products)
6. Quality maintenance (process-equipment links)
7. Safety, health, environment
8. TPM in administration (support functions)
```

In pharmaceutical GMP environments, pillars 2, 6, and 7 are particularly critical: planned maintenance integrates into production windows, quality maintenance monitors CPPs, and safety integrates HPAPI/GMP requirements.

## 4.3 Scheduling and resource conflict management

Planning maintenance interventions in a multi-department environment generates recurring resource conflicts:

| Conflict type | Parties involved | Arbitration |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Utility shutdown during production | Utilities vs Process | Advance planning, validated time slots |
| Calibration of instrument in service | Calibration vs Process | Replacement instrument, negotiated delay |
| Preventive shutdown vs batch in progress | Maintenance vs Process | Shutdown windows integrated into production schedule |
| Shared technician resources | Maintenance vs Calibration | Priority matrix, manager escalation |

Resolving these conflicts requires a **structured meeting mechanism** (daily/weekly maintenance-production meeting) and clear **escalation rules** documented in SOPs.

## 4.4 Oracle as coordination platform

Oracle CMMS plays a role as a **coordination platform** beyond its pure CMMS usage:

- Generated WOs are visible to all concerned departments
- Statuses (planned, in progress, completed) are updated in real time
- Intervention histories by equipment are accessible to Process and Calibration
- Automatic notifications inform stakeholders of planned interventions

This shared transparency reduces inter-departmental friction and strengthens mutual trust between teams.

## 4.5 Formal and informal communication

Effective coordination relies on two complementary channels:

**Formal:** weekly maintenance/production meetings, monthly KPI reports, Oracle records, coordination SOPs
**Informal:** field presence, availability, responsiveness to urgent requests, trust built over time

Modgil & Sharma (2016) emphasize that **"Total Productive Maintenance and Total Quality Management together have a significant positive impact on operational performance"** — a convergence that is only possible if both functions share common indicators and an established coordination language.
